SUN

INTRODUCTION
The Sun, the star at the center of our solar system, holds everything in its orbit through its gravity, from massive planets to tiny debris. This gravitational force influences Earth's seasons, ocean currents, weather, climate, and phenomena like radiation belts and auroras. While essential to us, the Sun is just one of billions of similar stars in the Milky Way galaxy.

ORBIT
The Sun,with a radius of 435,000 miles (700,000 km), is much larger and more massive than Earth. It would take over 330,000 Earths to match its mass and 1.3 million Earths to fill its volume. The Sun is 93 million miles (150 million km) from Earth, and its closest stellar neighbor is the Alpha Centauri system, with Proxima Centauri 4.24 light-years away and Alpha Centauri A and B 4.37 light-years away. One light-year equals about 6 trillion miles (9.5 trillion km).

SIZE
The Sun, a 4.5 billion-year-old yellow dwarf star made of hot hydrogen and helium, sits at the center of our solar system. Located about 93 million miles (150 million kilometers) from Earth, it's the only star in our solar system. The Sun's energy is essential for life on Earth, making our existence possible.

STRUCTURE
The Sun is a massive ball of hydrogen and helium held together by gravity. It has several regions: the core, radiative zone, and convection zone inside, followed by the photosphere, chromosphere, transition zone, and the corona outside. The corona produces the solar wind, forming the heliosphere, which extends beyond the planets.
The core, at 27 million °F (15 million °C), powers the Sun through nuclear fusion of hydrogen into helium. Energy travels outward through the radiative and convection zones, with plasma rising to the photosphere, the Sun's visible surface.

The sun is about 100 times wider than earth and 10 times wider than Jupiter the largest planet in the solar system
